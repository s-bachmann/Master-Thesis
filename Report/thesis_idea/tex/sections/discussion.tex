\subsection{Velocity space-time plots}
The main wavefronts which can be observed in figures \ref{fig:sub_vel} and \ref{fig:super_vel} are the crack fronts. This is because when taking the slope of these two wavefronts, it corresponds to the rupture velocity in the supershear and the sub-rayleigh case. \par

The weakening of the wavefronts, especially observed in the supershear case in figure \ref{fig:super_vel} can be explained of attenuation of the different frequencies which are present in the medium. Higher frequencies are attenuated much faster than low frequencies \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. The attenuation coefficient is \begin{equation}
\alpha(\omega) = \frac{\omega}{2 c Q}
\label{eq:attenuation_coeff}
\end{equation} \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}, which can be used to define the spatial amplitude decay: \begin{equation}
A(x, \omega) = A_0 e^{-\alpha(\omega) x} = A_0 e^{-\frac{\omega x}{2 c Q}}
\label{eq:amplitude_decay}
\end{equation} \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. This means that higher frequencies present in the wave train decay in amplitude faster than others with increasing distance. The resulting wavefield is described by the equation \begin{equation}
u(x, t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} U_0(\omega) e^{-\frac{\omega x}{2 c Q}} e^{i \omega \left(\frac{x}{c} - t\right)} d\omega
\label{eq:wavefield_integral}
\end{equation} \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. This is only present in the supershear case, because the crack travels faster than the guided waves \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}. A Mach-front is created, where all the waves travel together in a "shock-front" \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}, but once this front passes, there is attenuation of energy. In the sub-rayleigh case on the other hand, the guided waves are faster than the crack \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}, there is reflection of the waves between the top and bottom of the snow, which creates the osicllations which can be seen in figure \ref{fig:sub_vel}. \par

The broadening of the wavefront with increasing time can be explained by dispersion. By the reflections of the top and bottom snow boundary, the waves present start interfering with each other, which can lead to constructive or destructive interference \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. This means that waves can start travelling at different phase velocities, because of the interference \cite{graffWaveMotionElastic1975}. The phase and group velocities of waves are described as \begin{equation}
v_{\text{ph}} = \frac{\omega}{k}, \quad v_{\text{g}} = \frac{d\omega}{dk}
\label{eq:velocities}.
\end{equation} The interference leads to the broadening of the wavefront with increasing time, described by the envelope spreading law \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981} \begin{equation}
\Delta x(t) = \Delta x_0 \sqrt{1 + \left( \frac{\frac{d^2\omega}{dk^2} t}{(\Delta x_0)^2} \right)^2}
\label{eq:envelope_spreading}.
\end{equation} As time goes on, there are more reflections present, so there is more potential for interference and thus dispersion. \par

The presence of waves with opposite polarity can be proven mathematically. From the equation of motion in an elastic solid, which is \begin{equation}
    \mu \nabla^2 u + (\lambda + \mu) \nabla (\nabla \cdot u) = \rho \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}
    \label{eq:motion},
  \end{equation} the scalar and vector potential for P- and S-waves (Potentials $\ \phi, \psi$)can be derived using the Helmholtz-Decomposition and the displacement becomes \begin{equation}
    u = \nabla \phi + \nabla \times \psi. 
  \end{equation} Splitting this into the horizontal and vertical component of displacement: \begin{equation}
    u_x = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial z}
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    u_z = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}.
  \end{equation} When these are substituted into \ref{eq:motion}, it becomes \begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \phi = \frac{1}{c_p^2}\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}
  \end{equation} for P-waves and \begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \psi = \frac{1}{c_s^2} \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial t^2}
  \end{equation} for S-waves, the wave speeds are derived from the Lamé-Parameters, as described in \ref{sec:czm_methods}. \par

  When assuming harmonic plane wave solutions for waves with wavenumber k and frequency $\ \omega$, the harmonic solutions have the form \begin{equation}
    \phi(x,z,t) = f(z)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \psi(x,z,t) = g(z)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
  \end{equation}. When these are substituted into the wave equations, f(z) and g(z) yield ordinary differental equations, which are \begin{equation}
    \frac{d^2f}{dz^2} + p^2f = 0
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \frac{d^2g}{dz^2} + q^2g = 0 ,
  \end{equation} with $\ p^2 =  \frac{\omega^2}{c_p^s} -k^2$ and $\ q^2 =  \frac{\omega^2}{c_s^s} -k^2$. These equations lead to the general solutions \begin{equation}
    \phi = [Acos(pz) + Bsin(pz)]e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \psi = [Ccos(qz) + Dsin(qz)]e^{i(kx-\omega t)}.
  \end{equation} \par


  At the snow-air interface, there is a stress-free boundary because of the impedance contrast, accoding to \cite{igelComputationalSeismologyPractical2016}. This means that both the normal and the shear stress need to be zero: \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \lambda \nabla^2\phi + 2\mu \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial z} = 0
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu (\frac{\partial u_x}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial x}) = 0.
  \end{equation} These can be expressed in terms of the scalar and vector potential using the displacement potentials. \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \lambda (\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial z^2}) + 2\mu (\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial z^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x \partial z}),
  \end{equation} \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu (\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 \psi }{\partial z^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x \partial z} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}),
  \end{equation} of which the first terms can be substituted with the wavespeed using the wavenumber-frequency and velocity relationship from the Lamé parameters. This results in \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \mu [(q^2 -k^2) \phi + \frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x \partial z}]
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu [2 \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x \partial z} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} - \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial z^2}], 
  \end{equation} from which harmonic solutions containing sin and cos can be obtained. Here, a derivative is a multiplication with ik, which leads to \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \mu [(q^2- k^2) \phi + 2ik \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial z}]
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu [2ik \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} + (q^2 - k^2)\psi].
  \end{equation} When taking into account the stress free boundary and substituting $\ K = (q^2 - k^2)$, then the normal stress at ±z becomes \begin{equation}
    \sigma_n = K [A cos (p±z) + Bsin(p±z) + 2ik (-q(sin(q±z) + qDcos(p±z)))] = 0
  \end{equation} and for the shear stress \begin{equation}
    \sigma_s = 2ik[-pAsin(p±z) + pBcos(p±z)] + K[Ccos(q±z) + Dsin(q±z)] = 0
  \end{equation} at the top and botto boundary of the snow layer. The matrix for symmetric Lamb waves is obtained by adding the top and bottom term of the normal stress and subtracting the shear stresses. This leads to \begin{equation}
    2KAcos(pz) + 4ikqDcos(qz) =
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    -4ikpAsin(pz) + 2KDsin(qz) =
  \end{equation}, which can be written as a matrix: \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
      Kcos(pz) & 2ikqcos(qz) \\
      -2ikpsin(pz) & Ksin(qz)
    \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}
      A \\ D
    \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
      0 \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}.
  \end{equation} If the determinant of this matrix is set to zero, this results in \begin{equation}
    \frac{tan(qz)}{tan(pz)} = -\frac{2k^2pq}{(q^2-k^2)^2}
  \end{equation}, which is the dispersion of the symmetric mode of the Lamb waves, as described by \cite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. The same can be done for the asymmetric Lamb wave mode if the operations of the stresses are opposite. \par

  The presence of opposite polarities across both velocity components ($v_x, v_y$) and across both propagation regimes (sub-Rayleigh and supershear) is a direct consequence of the waveguide's geometric symmetry and the nature of the fracture source. Dynamically, the two recording depths, $y = 2.625\,\text{m}$ and $y = 2.25\,\text{m}$, are perfectly symmetric about the mid-plane of the snow slab ($y = 2.4375\,\text{m}$), mapping precisely to $\pm z$ in the analytical framework. 

The fact that this phase-reversal persists across both the sub-Rayleigh and supershear regimes demonstrates that while the rupture velocity changes the radiation pattern (e.g., generating dispersive wave trains versus non-dispersive Mach shock fronts), the underlying phase relationships remain strictly governed by the structural eigenfunctions of the plate waveguide. 

The presence of opposite polarities in both the horizontal and vertical velocity fields simultaneously indicates that the wavefield is not a single  mode, but rather a superposition of both symmetric ($u^{\text{sym}}$) and antisymmetric ($u^{\text{asym}}$) modes:
\begin{equation}
u_x(x, \pm z, t) = u_x^{\text{sym}}(x, \pm z, t) + u_x^{\text{asym}}(x, \pm z, t)
\label{eq:superposition_x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
u_z(x, \pm z, t) = u_z^{\text{sym}}(x, \pm z, t) + u_z^{\text{asym}}(x, \pm z, t)
\label{eq:superposition_z}
\end{equation}

Because a crack propagating along a weak layer acts as an asymmetric boundary source, it breaks the symmetry of the slab and couples energy into both structural types simultaneously. Evaluating Equations \ref{eq:superposition_x} and \ref{eq:superposition_z} at $+z$ versus $-z$ activates the opposing parities of the underlying $\sin$ and $\cos$ depth functions:
\begin{equation}
u_x(x, -z, t) = u_x^{\text{sym}}(x, z, t) - u_x^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t)
\label{eq:parity_flip_x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
u_z(x, -z, t) = -u_z^{\text{sym}}(x, z, t) + u_z^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t)
\label{eq:parity_flip_z}
\end{equation}

This modal interference shifts the cumulative phase balances, causing a reversal of wave-train polarity between the crack depth and the snowpack bottom for both components of motion \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. \par

  Lamb waves are elastic guided waves that occur in plates \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. They consist of particle motion in the direction of the wave polarization as well as the direction perpendicular to it \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. They are guided by the boundaries of the layer or media in which they propagate and have a symmetric and an asymmetric mode \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. The symmetric mode dominates the in-plate motion, while the antisymmetric mode dominates the vertical (out of plane motion ) \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. \par


\subsection{Strain and strain rate space-time plots}

The sub-rayleigh horizontal strain and strain rate plots in figure \ref{fig:sub_strain} look very similar to the velocity plots in figure \ref{fig:sub_vel}. The same is true for the supershear strain and strain rate in figure \ref{fig:super_strain}, which look similar to all but the vertical velocity at the snow bottom (top right in figure \ref{fig:super_vel}). This is intuitive because strain is the temporal derivative of displacement ($\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}$), strain rate is the temporal derivative of strain ($\ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}$, so the second temporal derivative of displacement). Velocity on the other hand is the spatial derivative of displacement ($\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}$), so all the values are derived from displacement. \par

\subsection{Discussion of fk-plot}

The narrower energy band on the positive side of the spectrum, as observed in the supershear case in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk} can be explained by source directivity. \cite{ben-menahemRadiationSeismicSurfacewaves1961} derive directionality factors for seismic waves propagating on either side of a fault. The directionality factor is \begin{equation}
D = \left| \frac{\left( \frac{C}{v} + \cos \theta_0 \right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} - \cos \theta_0 \right) \right]}{\left( \frac{C}{v} - \cos \theta_0 \right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} + \cos \theta_0 \right) \right]} \right|
\label{eq:directivity_func}
\end{equation} \parencite{ben-menahemRadiationSeismicSurfacewaves1961}. Analogous to the case described here, where the crack starts at one point in the medium, where the energy travels in the direction of the crack but also opposite to the direction of crack propagation. \cite{ben-menahemRadiationSeismicSurfacewaves1961} describe that energy is concentrated in the direction of propagation, so in the positive direction. When looking at the directivity function in equation \ref{eq:directivity_func} at 0 degrees (direction of propagation), the function becomes \begin{equation}
    D = \left| \frac{\left( \frac{C}{v} + 1 \right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} - 1 \right) \right]}{\left( \frac{C}{v} - 1\right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} + 1 \right) \right]} \right|
\end{equation}, so if the phase velocity (C) is close to the rupture velocity (v), this can lead to a concentration of energy. The opposite is true in the negative direction (180 degrees), where the amplitude is reduced. This explains why the energy is more concentrated and less smeared out in the positive direction compared to the negative direction. \par

The concentration between vp and the rupture speed in the supershear case can be explained by the radiation of energy. The rupture velocity in the medium exceeds the shear wave speed, so the s-waves no longer decay, they radiate away from the fault as Mach cones \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}. These waves have a certain angle which is described by \begin{equation}
    \xi = \sin^{-1} \left( \frac{c_s}{v} \right) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{\hat{\eta}_s} \right)
\end{equation}, so is determined by the ratio between the rupture speed and the s-wave speed \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}. These waves are tied to the rupture front, which can explain why the energy is concentrated between the p-wave speed and the rupture speed in the supershear case in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk}. The p-wave speed in this case is still faster than the rupture, so this energy outruns the rupture, which means the p-waves precede. The rupture and the Mach cone associated with it arrive second, which explains why the energy is concentrated between the two, according to \cite{rosakisIntersonicShearCracks2002}. \par

In the sub-rayleigh case, the energy is concentated at the p-wave speeds, which are the fastest speeds present in the medium. The energy is dominated by the starting and stopping phases, which are radiated by the abrupt starting and stopping of the rupture \parencite{madariagaHighfrequencyRadiationCrack1977}. These are governed by the material speeds, so the p- and s-wave speeds \parencite{madariagaHighfrequencyRadiationCrack1977}. The energy concentration observed at the negative rupture speed could be explained by evanescence of the waves \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}, which means that the amplitude of the waves decays exponentially with their distance to the fault. The furthest distance from the end of the fault is opposite to the direction of propagation, which means that the p- and s-wave energy has decayed the most there. This could allow energy from the fault to show up in the fk plot in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk} at that location because it is no longer overshadowed by the stronger energy of the propagating waves. This could also explain why this energy is only observed in the negative direction. \par

The strong contrast in how the energy is concentrated between the sub-rayleigh and supershear case in \ref{fig:preliminary_fk} can be explained by the number of locations the energy is concetrated. As described above, the supershear regime has two energy fronts, whereas the sub-rayleigh case has only one. This explains why the energy bands in the supershear case are much more broad compared to the sub-rayleigh case. \par 

The observation that at higher frequencies in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk}, the lines of the energy present do not match as well anymore with the concentration of energy, can be explained by numerical dispersion. As \cite{komatitschIntroductionSpectralElement1999} describe, as frequency increases, the wavelength decreases. This means that higher frequencies are not resloved as well by a spatial grid with a certain spacing \cite{yilmazSeismicDataAnalysis2001}, this starts bending the energy in the fk plot. This could explain the mismatch between the physical velocity lines and the concetrations of energy. \par
