\section{Simple Model}
\label{sec:simple_disc}

\subsection{Velocity space-time plots}
The main wavefronts which can be observed in figures \ref{fig:sub_vel} and \ref{fig:super_vel} are the crack fronts. This is because when taking the slope of these two wavefronts, it corresponds to the rupture velocity in the supershear and the sub-rayleigh case. \par

The precursor wave which can be observed in the vx plots in the sub-rayleigh case in figure \ref{fig:sub_vel} has a much faster speed than the rupture itself. This wave also only occurs in the sub-rayleigh case in the vx plots. Simulations with larger source spacing (for example 10m spacing instead of 0.1m spacing, see appendix \ref{app:D}, figure \ref{fig:wide_sub_vel}) have shown that this precursor disappears if source spacing is increased. This leads to the conclusion that the precursor to the main wavefront appears because of interference. All other sources but the first and the last one have interference from the sources before and after it, which means that this wave type, although emitted, is cancelled out because of interference \parencite{madariagaDynamicsExpandingCircular1976}. When the source spacing is increased, the fronts of the individual sources travel further apart in space and they interfere less. \par

The precursor wavefront is the symmetric Lamb wave mode (S0), whose propagation speed is limited by the plate wave speed: \begin{equation}
  c_p^{plate} = 2c_s\sqrt{1-\frac{v_s^2}{v_p^2}}
\end{equation} \parencite{roseWavesPlates2014}. When using the material parameters used in the simulation, this is just short of the p-wave speed of the slab. The speed of the S0 wave converges to the plate wave speed for low frequencies (long wavelength limit) \parencite{roseWavesPlates2014}, which is why this wave is believed to be the S0 mode which is excited. \par

This wave is only present in the sub-rayleigh regime because in the supershear case, the rupture front, S0 and the pressure wave all have velocities within a couple of meters per second within each other. This means that the wavefront could be present, but it cannot be seen because it arrives at the same time as all other waves in the medium. \par

The weakening of the wavefronts, especially observed in the supershear case in figure \ref{fig:super_vel} can be explained by depth decay and geometric spreading from the source. The crack tip acts as a moving two-dimensional line source, in a 2D elastic medium, the peak amplitude $A$ of the radiated wavefield decays inversely with the square root of the radial distance $r$ from the source plane \parencite{achenbachWavePropagationElastic2012}:
\begin{equation}
A(r) = \frac{A_0}{\sqrt{r}}
\label{eq:geometric_spreading_2d}
\end{equation}
where $r = \sqrt{(x - x_0)^2 + (y - y_{\text{src}})^2}$. As a consequence, as the wavefield travels vertically through the slab, away from the crack plane, towards the snow bottom, it experiences a geometric reduction in peak energy. \par

Furthermore, for wave components whose horizontal phase velocities are slower than the bulk wave speeds of the snow, the vertical wavenumbers become purely imaginary. Defining $p = i\alpha$ and $q = i\beta$, where $\alpha = \sqrt{k^2 - (\omega/c_p)^2}$ and $\beta = \sqrt{k^2 - (\omega/c_s)^2}$ \parencite{graffWaveMotionElastic1975}, the trigonometric expressions in the wave potentials transform into hyperbolic functions:
\begin{equation}
\phi(x,z,t) = \left[ A \cosh(\alpha z) + B \sinh(\alpha z) \right] e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
\label{eq:hyperbolic_phi}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\psi(x,z,t) = \left[ C \cosh(\beta z) + D \sinh(\beta z) \right] e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
\label{eq:hyperbolic_psi}
\end{equation}
These hyperbolic profiles dictate that high-frequency, shorter wavelength  phases decay exponentially as a function of vertical distance from the location of the crack. In other words, higher frequencies are attenuated more quickly, which explains the weakening of the wavefronts observed. \par


This is only present in the supershear case, because the crack travels faster than the guided waves \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}. A Mach-front is created, where all the waves travel together in a "shock-front" \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}, but once this front passes, there is attenuation of energy. In the sub-rayleigh case on the other hand, the guided waves are faster than the crack \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}, there is reflection of the waves between the top and bottom of the snow, which creates the osicllations which can be seen in figure \ref{fig:sub_vel}. \par

The broadening of the wavefront with increasing time can be explained by dispersion. By the reflections of the top and bottom snow boundary, the waves present start interfering with each other, which can lead to constructive or destructive interference \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. This means that waves can start travelling at different phase velocities, because of the interference \cite{graffWaveMotionElastic1975}. The phase and group velocities of waves are described as \begin{equation}
v_{\text{ph}} = \frac{\omega}{k}, \quad v_{\text{g}} = \frac{d\omega}{dk}
\label{eq:velocities}.
\end{equation} The interference leads to the broadening of the wavefront with increasing time, described by the envelope spreading law \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981} \begin{equation}
\Delta x(t) = \Delta x_0 \sqrt{1 + \left( \frac{\frac{d^2\omega}{dk^2} t}{(\Delta x_0)^2} \right)^2}
\label{eq:envelope_spreading}.
\end{equation} As time goes on, there are more reflections present, so there is more potential for interference and thus dispersion. \par

The presence of waves with opposite polarity can be proven mathematically. From the equation of motion in an elastic solid, which is \begin{equation}
    \mu \nabla^2 u + (\lambda + \mu) \nabla (\nabla \cdot u) = \rho \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}
    \label{eq:motion},
  \end{equation} the scalar and vector potential for P- and S-waves (Potentials $\ \phi, \psi$)  can be derived using the Helmholtz-Decomposition and the displacement becomes \begin{equation}
    u = \nabla \phi + \nabla \times \psi. 
  \end{equation} Splitting this into the horizontal and vertical component of displacement: \begin{equation}
    u_x = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial z}
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    u_z = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}.
  \end{equation} When these are substituted into \ref{eq:motion}, it becomes \begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \phi = \frac{1}{c_p^2}\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}
  \end{equation} for P-waves and \begin{equation}
    \nabla^2 \psi = \frac{1}{c_s^2} \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial t^2}
  \end{equation} for S-waves, the wave speeds are derived from the Lamé-Parameters, as described in \ref{sec:czm_methods}. \par

  When assuming harmonic plane wave solutions for waves with wavenumber k and frequency $\ \omega$, the harmonic solutions have the form \begin{equation}
    \phi(x,z,t) = f(z)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \psi(x,z,t) = g(z)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
  \end{equation}. When these are substituted into the wave equations, f(z) and g(z) yield ordinary differental equations, which are \begin{equation}
    \frac{d^2f}{dz^2} + p^2f = 0
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \frac{d^2g}{dz^2} + q^2g = 0 ,
  \end{equation} with $\ p^2 =  \frac{\omega^2}{c_p^s} -k^2$ and $\ q^2 =  \frac{\omega^2}{c_s^s} -k^2$. These equations lead to the general solutions \begin{equation}
    \phi = [Acos(pz) + Bsin(pz)]e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \psi = [Ccos(qz) + Dsin(qz)]e^{i(kx-\omega t)}.
  \end{equation} \par


  At the snow-air interface, there is a stress-free boundary because of the impedance contrast, accoding to \cite{igelComputationalSeismologyPractical2016}. This means that both the normal and the shear stress need to be zero: \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \lambda \nabla^2\phi + 2\mu \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial z} = 0
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu (\frac{\partial u_x}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial u_z}{\partial x}) = 0.
  \end{equation} These can be expressed in terms of the scalar and vector potential using the displacement potentials. \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \lambda (\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial z^2}) + 2\mu (\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial z^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x \partial z}),
  \end{equation} \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu (\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 \psi }{\partial z^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x \partial z} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}),
  \end{equation} of which the first terms can be substituted with the wavespeed using the wavenumber-frequency and velocity relationship from the Lamé parameters. This results in \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \mu [(q^2 -k^2) \phi + \frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x \partial z}]
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu [2 \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x \partial z} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} - \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial z^2}], 
  \end{equation} from which harmonic solutions containing sin and cos can be obtained. Here, a derivative is a multiplication with ik, which leads to \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{zz} = \mu [(q^2- k^2) \phi + 2ik \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial z}]
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    \sigma_{xz} = \mu [2ik \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} + (q^2 - k^2)\psi].
  \end{equation} When taking into account the stress free boundary and substituting $\ K = (q^2 - k^2)$, then the normal stress at $\ ±z$ becomes \begin{equation}
    \sigma_n = K [A cos (p±z) + Bsin(p±z) + 2ik (-q(sin(q±z) + qDcos(p±z)))] = 0
  \end{equation} and for the shear stress \begin{equation}
    \sigma_s = 2ik[-pAsin(p±z) + pBcos(p±z)] + K[Ccos(q±z) + Dsin(q±z)] = 0
  \end{equation} at the top and botto boundary of the snow layer. The matrix for symmetric Lamb waves is obtained by adding the top and bottom term of the normal stress and subtracting the shear stresses. This leads to \begin{equation}
    2KAcos(pz) + 4ikqDcos(qz) =
  \end{equation} and \begin{equation}
    -4ikpAsin(pz) + 2KDsin(qz) =
  \end{equation}, which can be written as a matrix: \begin{equation}
    \begin{pmatrix}
      Kcos(pz) & 2ikqcos(qz) \\
      -2ikpsin(pz) & Ksin(qz)
    \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}
      A \\ D
    \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
      0 \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}.
  \end{equation} If the determinant of this matrix is set to zero, this results in \begin{equation}
    \frac{tan(qz)}{tan(pz)} = -\frac{2k^2pq}{(q^2-k^2)^2}
    \label{eq:symmetric dispersion}
  \end{equation}, which is the dispersion of the symmetric mode of the Lamb waves, as described by \cite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. The same can be done for the asymmetric Lamb wave mode if the operations of the stresses are opposite. \par


The presence of opposite polarities in both the horizontal and vertical velocity fields indicates that the wavefield is not a single  mode, but rather a superposition of both symmetric ($u^{\text{sym}}$) and antisymmetric ($u^{\text{asym}}$) modes:
\begin{equation}
u_x(x, \pm z, t) = u_x^{\text{sym}}(x, \pm z, t) + u_x^{\text{asym}}(x, \pm z, t)
\label{eq:superposition_x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
u_z(x, \pm z, t) = u_z^{\text{sym}}(x, \pm z, t) + u_z^{\text{asym}}(x, \pm z, t).
\label{eq:superposition_z}
\end{equation}

Because a crack propagating along a weak layer acts as an asymmetric boundary source, it breaks the symmetry of the slab and couples energy into both Lamb wave types at the same time. Evaluating Equations \ref{eq:superposition_x} and \ref{eq:superposition_z} at $+z$ versus $-z$ activates the opposing parities (even and odd value) of the underlying $\sin$ and $\cos$ depth functions:
\begin{equation}
u_x(x, -z, t) = u_x^{\text{sym}}(x, z, t) - u_x^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t)
\label{eq:parity_flip_x}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
u_z(x, -z, t) = -u_z^{\text{sym}}(x, z, t) + u_z^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t).
\label{eq:parity_flip_z}
\end{equation}

  Lamb waves are elastic guided waves that occur in plates \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. They consist of particle motion in the direction of the wave polarization as well as the direction perpendicular to it \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. They are guided by the boundaries of the layer or media in which they propagate and have a symmetric and an asymmetric mode \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. The symmetric mode dominates the in-plate motion, while the antisymmetric mode dominates the vertical (out of plane motion ) \parencite{suFundamentalsAnalysisLamb2009}. \par


\subsection{Strain and strain rate space-time plots}

The sub-rayleigh horizontal strain and strain rate plots in figure \ref{fig:sub_strain} look very similar to the velocity plots in figure \ref{fig:sub_vel}. The same is true for the supershear strain and strain rate in figure \ref{fig:super_strain}, which look similar to all but the vertical velocity at the snow bottom (top right in figure \ref{fig:super_vel}). This is intuitive because strain is the spatial derivative of displacement ($\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}$), strain rate is the temporal derivative of strain ($\ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x \partial t}$). Velocity on the other hand is the spatial derivative of displacement ($\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}$), so all the values are derived from displacement. The first wave caused by lack of interference \parencite{madariagaHighfrequencyRadiationCrack1977} in the sub-rayleigh case can also be observed in the strain and strain rate plots, this disappears again when source spacing is increased (appendix \ref{app:D}, figure \ref{fig:wide_sub_strain}). \par


Both the sub-rayleigh and the supershear strain and strain rate cases show a wave train running ahead. The slope of the first wavefront observed in figures \ref{fig:sub_strain,fig:super_strain} is close to the p-wave velocity in snow and the rupture velocity in the supershear case, which as discussed, is also close to the velocity of the S0 mode. This means that in both cases, the S0 wave modes are the first arrivals because they are fastest, likely followed by the p-waves. This is consistent with the observations made in the velocity plots. In the supershear case, the S0 waves are followed immediately by the waves from the rupture front, and the p-waves which would explain why there is only one clear but very broad wavefront in figure \ref{fig:super_strain}. In the sub-rayleigh strain and strain rate plots in figure \ref{fig:sub_strain}, the observations are also consistent with velocity. The first wave front to arrive are the p-waves and the S0 mode, followed by the front from the rupture and the s-waves last. The same goes for the widening of the wavefronts, which has already been discussed in the velocity section. \par 

The first arrival of the p-wave and S0 mode is also consistent with wave propagation. All plots in figures \ref{fig:sub_strain,fig:super_strain} show positive horizontal strain or strain rate first, followed by compression (negative strain) and positive strain again. This propagation is characterized by extension first and then contraction \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. \par

This very repetitive sequence of opposite polarities is a consequence of the snow acting as a waveguide, as described above. When energy is suddenly released by a propagating crack tip, the crack acts as an impulsive source that projects disturbances into the overlying slab \parencite{heierliAnticrackNucleationTriggering2008, gaumeDynamicAnticrackPropagation2018}. The leading P-wave arrival corresponds to the high-frequency, long-wavelength limit of the symmetric ($S_0$) Lamb mode, which represents an in-plane, extensional push-pull wave packet \parencite{achenbachWavePropagationElastic2012, graffWaveMotionElastic1975}. As this transient $S_0$ pulse outruns the rupture front, the initial forward displacement stretches the material, yielding a leading zone of positive tensile strain (red color). To satisfy elastodynamic restoring forces and conserve momentum as the wavefront passes a fixed point in space, this  extension must be immediately followed by a compensating contractional phase of negative compressive strain (blue), before returning to equilibrium.

Behind this fast, extensional precursor lies the slower, highly oscillatory wave train, which displays a dense pattern of alternating opposite polarities. This trailing pattern is dictated by the superposition of the symmetric mode as well as the antisymmetric ($A_0$) flexural Lamb wave mode. Similar to the dispersion of the symmetric mode of the Lamb waves in equation \ref{eq:symmetric dispersion}, the antisymmetric wave mode can be derived. For the antisymmetric mode, the vertical displacement must be an even function of depth, $u_z(-z) = u_z(z)$, while the horizontal displacement must be an odd function, $u_x(-z) = -u_x(z)$. To satisfy the geometric properties, the integration constants representing the even component of $\phi$ and the odd component of $\psi$ must vanish, such that $A = 0$ and $D = 0$. The asymmetric potentials simplify to:
\begin{equation}
\phi = B\sin(pz)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
\label{eq:phi_asym}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\psi = C\cos(qz)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}.
\label{eq:psi_asym}
\end{equation}

Substituting Equations \ref{eq:phi_asym} and \ref{eq:psi_asym} into the stress-free boundary equations ($\sigma_{zz} = 0, \sigma_{xz} = 0$) at the free surfaces $\pm z$ yields the following homogeneous matrix system:
\begin{equation}
\begin{pmatrix}
K\sin(pz) & -2ikq\sin(qz) \\
2ikp\cos(pz) & K\cos(qz)
\end{pmatrix} 
\begin{pmatrix} B \\ C \end{pmatrix} = 
\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}
\label{eq:matrix_asym}
\end{equation}
where $K = (q^2 - k^2)$. Setting the determinant of the matrix to zero gives the characteristic dispersion relation for the antisymmetric Lamb wave modes:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\tan(pz)}{\tan(qz)} = \frac{4k^2pq}{(q^2-k^2)^2}
\label{eq:dispersion_asym}
\end{equation}

To relate this explicitly to the strain, the horizontal displacement $u_x$ is computed from the potentials:
\begin{equation}
u_x = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial z} = \left[ ik B\sin(pz) + q C\sin(qz) \right] e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
\label{eq:ux_asym}
\end{equation}

Taking the spatial derivative with respect to $x$ to find the horizontal strain ($\varepsilon_{xx} = \partial u_x / \partial x$) transforms the expression via multiplication by $ik$ in the wavenumber domain:
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_{xx}^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t) = \left[ -k^2 B\sin(pz) + ikq C\sin(qz) \right] e^{i(kx-\omega t)}
\label{eq:strain_asym_final}
\end{equation}

By isolating the real part of this analytical expression, the spatial and temporal variation of the flexural strain field isolates the reason for the opposite polarity patterns along the wave train. The strain can be rewritten in terms of its harmonic phase:
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_{xx}^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t) = \mathcal{A}_{\text{asym}}(z) \cos(kx - \omega t + \phi_0)
\label{eq:harmonic_strain_real}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}_{\text{asym}}(z)$ represents the depth-dependent amplitude envelope and $\phi_0$ is a constant phase angle. The transcendental cosine term dictates that over a fixed spatial distance of exactly half a wavelength, $\Delta x = \lambda/2 = \pi/k$, the wavefield must cycle smoothly between its maximum positive value ($\cos = +1$) and its maximum negative value ($\cos = -1$). 

Physically, this represents the dynamic bending or structural "ringing" of the slab  \parencite{heierliAnticrackNucleationTriggering2008, bergfeldSignaturesSubRayleighSupershear2025}. When a section of the slab flexes vertically, the fibers located on a single side of the neutral axis experience localized horizontal squeezing ($\varepsilon_{xx} < 0$, blue bands). As the elastic wave oscillates into its next half-cycle, the local curvature reverses, subjecting those same fibers to intense horizontal stretching ($\varepsilon_{xx} > 0$, red bands). 

When this is translated to the horizontal strain rate ($\dot{\varepsilon}_{xx}$), the time derivative ($\partial/\partial t \longleftrightarrow -i\omega$) shifts the wave profile into a sine function:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\varepsilon}_{xx}^{\text{asym}}(x, z, t) = \omega \mathcal{A}_{\text{asym}}(z) \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi_0)
\label{eq:harmonic_rate_real}
\end{equation}
This differentiation scales the amplitude by the angular frequency $\omega$ and introduces a $90^\circ$ spatial phase shift, but leaves the underlying alternating pattern of opposite polarities completely intact. Because both the crack depth ($y = 2.625\,\text{m}$) and the snowpack base ($y = 2.25\,\text{m}$) reside entirely within the lower half of the slab waveguide below its neutral axis ($z < 0$), they evaluate to the same geometric sign in $\sin(pz)$. Consequently, both recording horizons register these rapid, cyclic transitions from extension to contraction perfectly in-phase with one another. 



\subsection{Discussion of fk-plot}

The narrower energy band on the positive side of the spectrum, as observed in the supershear case in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk} can be explained by source directivity. \cite{ben-menahemRadiationSeismicSurfacewaves1961} derive directionality factors for seismic waves propagating on either side of a fault. The directionality factor is \begin{equation}
D = \left| \frac{\left( \frac{C}{v} + \cos \theta_0 \right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} - \cos \theta_0 \right) \right]}{\left( \frac{C}{v} - \cos \theta_0 \right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} + \cos \theta_0 \right) \right]} \right|
\label{eq:directivity_func}
\end{equation} \parencite{ben-menahemRadiationSeismicSurfacewaves1961}. Analogous to the case described here, where the crack starts at one point in the medium, where the energy travels in the direction of the crack but also opposite to the direction of crack propagation. \cite{ben-menahemRadiationSeismicSurfacewaves1961} describe that energy is concentrated in the direction of propagation, so in the positive direction. When looking at the directivity function in equation \ref{eq:directivity_func} at 0 degrees (direction of propagation), the function becomes \begin{equation}
    D = \left| \frac{\left( \frac{C}{v} + 1 \right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} - 1 \right) \right]}{\left( \frac{C}{v} - 1\right) \sin \left[ \frac{\pi b}{\lambda} \left( \frac{C}{v} + 1 \right) \right]} \right|,
\end{equation} so if the phase velocity (C) is close to the rupture velocity (v), this can lead to a concentration of energy. The opposite is true in the negative direction (180 degrees), where the amplitude is reduced. This explains why the energy is more concentrated and less smeared out in the positive direction compared to the negative direction. \par

The concentration between S0, vp and the rupture speed in the supershear case can be explained by the radiation of energy. The rupture velocity in the medium exceeds the shear wave speed, so the s-waves no longer decay, they radiate away from the fault as Mach cones \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}. These waves have a certain angle which is described by \begin{equation}
    \xi = \sin^{-1} \left( \frac{c_s}{v} \right) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{\hat{\eta}_s} \right),
\end{equation} so is determined by the ratio between the rupture speed and the s-wave speed \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}. These waves are tied to the rupture front, which can explain why the energy is concentrated between the p-wave speed and the rupture speed in the supershear case in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk}. The p-wave speed in this case is still faster than the rupture, so this energy outruns the rupture, which means the p-waves precede. The rupture and the Mach cone associated with it arrive second, which explains why the energy is concentrated between the two, according to \cite{rosakisIntersonicShearCracks2002}. \par

In the sub-rayleigh case, the energy is concentated at the S0 and p-wave speeds, which are the fastest speeds present in the medium. The energy is dominated by the starting and stopping phases, which are radiated by the abrupt starting and stopping of the rupture \parencite{madariagaHighfrequencyRadiationCrack1977}. These are governed by the material speeds, so the p- and s-wave speeds \parencite{madariagaHighfrequencyRadiationCrack1977,roseWavesPlates2014}. The energy concentration observed at the negative rupture speed could be explained by evanescence of the waves \parencite{dunhamNearsourceGroundMotion2005}, which means that the amplitude of the waves decays exponentially with their distance to the fault. The furthest distance from the end of the fault is opposite to the direction of propagation, which means that the p- and s-wave energy has decayed the most there. This could allow energy from the fault to show up in the fk plot in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk} at that location because it is no longer overshadowed by the stronger energy of the propagating waves. This could also explain why this energy is only observed in the negative direction. \par

The strong contrast in how the energy is concentrated between the sub-rayleigh and supershear case in \ref{fig:preliminary_fk} can be explained by the number of locations the energy is concetrated. As described above, the supershear regime has two energy fronts, whereas the sub-rayleigh case has only one. This explains why the energy bands in the supershear case are much more broad compared to the sub-rayleigh case. \par 

The observation that at higher frequencies in figure \ref{fig:preliminary_fk}, the lines of the energy present do not match as well anymore with the concentration of energy, can be explained by numerical dispersion. As \cite{komatitschIntroductionSpectralElement1999} describe, as frequency increases, the wavelength decreases. This means that higher frequencies are not resloved as well by a spatial grid with a certain spacing \cite{yilmazSeismicDataAnalysis2001}, this starts bending the energy in the fk plot. This could explain the mismatch between the physical velocity lines and the concetrations of energy. \par
