\section{Spectral Element Wave Propagation Modeling}
\label{sec:salvus_method}

The model described in \ref{sec:czm_methods} is implemented using the finite-element (FE) method in SALVUS. The following section describes the physics used by SALVUS. Understanding the phsysics behind the modeling has relevance for understanding and interpreting the results, but also what can be modeled. To discretize a domain, the domain is divided into a finite number of elements \parencite{FiniteElementMethod}. The number of elements in this case is defined by the maximum wave frequency that needs to be resolved accurately, the number of mesh elements per wavelength is user-input. The boundaries of these elements describe the mesh, the mesh has a finite number of grid-points (edge points of the elements) \parencite{FiniteElementMethod}. The governing equations are determined at every element node, so for rectangular elements with nodes 1,2,3 and 4, there is the system $\ r^1 = \begin{pmatrix}r_1^1 \\ r_2^1 \\ r_3^1\\ r_4^1 \end{pmatrix}$ for force  and $\ u^1 = \begin{pmatrix}u_1^1 \\ u_2^1 \\ u_3^1\\ u_4^1 \end{pmatrix}$ for displacement \parencite{FiniteElementMethod}. The residual or characteristic relationship of linear elastic elements is of the form $\ r^1 = f^1-K^1u^1 $, where f are the nodal forces distributing load on the nodes and K is the stiffness matrix of a specific element \parencite{FiniteElementMethod}. In FE, the approximation of the wavefield by basis functions leads to a representation over the whole domain and not just at a set of points \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. This means integrals can be calculated over surfaces or even volumes \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. \par

Snow is a porous medium \parencite{mellorEngineeringPropertiesSnow1977}, which is approximated as a solid in this Thesis. This is because SALVUS meshes need an isotropic, solid material and cannot account for pore spaces. Higher frequencies in the resulting data would be atteunated much quicker in an actual porous medium \parencite{koushikDevelopmentSeismicSignalbased2024}. In addition, the presence of pore spaces in the medium would lead to complex scattering of the fields in the results, their absence is an important factor to consider when comparing simulated data to real-world data. The presence of air or water in snow is not neglected completely in the SALVUS simulations implemented however, because the material properties taken from \cite{guillemotEffectSnowfallChanges2021} are based on field measurements of snow. This means that the velocities measured by \cite{guillemotEffectSnowfallChanges2021} are not of pure snow, but snow that contains air or water. \par

The governing equation considered in this case is the elastic wave equation, because real-life crack propagation emits P- and S-waves. The wave equation for a heterogenous elastic solid in the n-dimensional domain G is 
\begin{equation}
  \rho\partial_t^2u = \nabla \cdot \sigma + f
  \label{eq:waveeq simple}
\end{equation} \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019,kAkiRichards19801981}. Stress can be related to displacement using Hoek's law: $\ \sigma = \frac{C}{\nabla u}$, where C is a 4th order tensor which characterizes the stiffness of the medium \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. Combining \ref{eq:waveeq simple} with Hook's law, this leads to \begin{equation}
\rho\partial_t^2u = \nabla \cdot \frac{C}{\nabla u} + f,
\label{eq:strong form}
\end{equation} which is the strong form of the linear elastic wave equation. \par


Boundary conditions, such as free surfaces, as well as initial conditions must be stated explicitly, they have the form $\ \sigma \cdot \hat{n}|_{\partial_G} = \bar{\tau}(x), u|_{t=0} = \bar{u}(x), v|_{t=0} = \bar{v}(x)$ \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. In this case, $\ \bar{\tau}(x), \bar{u}(x), \bar{v}(x) $ are the spatially variable distributions of traction, displacement and velocity \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. The outward pointing normal vector on the boundary of domain G is described by $\ \hat{n}|_{\partial G}$. \par

FE methods use the weak form of the governing equations \parencite{FiniteElementMethod}. To obtain the weak form of the linear elastic wave equation in \ref{eq:waveeq simple}, the whole equation needs to be multiplied with a test function w, which is not time-dependent \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. The weak form enforces the solution of the governing equation across the domain, instead of solving the equation everywhere, an integration over time leads to \begin{equation}
  \int_{G} \rho w \cdot \partial_t^2u\, d^n x = \int_G w \cdot (\nabla \cdot \sigma)\, d^n x + \int_G w \cdot f\, d^n x
  \label{eq: first weak form }
\end{equation} \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. The divergence theorem relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the divergence of the field in an enclosed volume: $\ \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot F) dV = \oiint_s (F \cdot \hat{n}) dS $, where F is the vector field. When this is applied to the first term on the right in \ref{eq: first weak form }, this leads to \begin{equation}
  \int_{G} \rho w \cdot \partial_t^2 u\, d^n x = \int_{\partial G} w \cdot (\sigma \cdot \hat{n})\, d^{n-1}x - \int_G (\nabla w : \sigma)\, d^n x + \int_G w \cdot f\, d^n x
  \label{eq:second weak form}
\end{equation} \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. The first integral on the left side of \ref{eq:second weak form} is the boundary integral, which changes depending on the boundary conditions implemented. The inegral on the right hand side is the mass term, the second on the left hand side is the stiffness term, and the last on the left hand side is the external forcing term, all in \ref{eq:second weak form}. Equation \ref{eq:second weak form} can be compacted to \begin{equation}
  (\rho\partial_t^2u,w)_G - \langle \sigma \cdot \hat{n}, w \rangle_{\partial G} + (\sigma, \nabla w)_G = (f,w)_G,
  \label{eq:compact weak form}
\end{equation} using the notations $\ (a,b)_G = \int_g a \cdot b d^nx, \langle a,b\rangle_{\partial_G} = \int_{\partial G}  a \cdot b d^{n-1}x$  as well as Hoek's law \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. This can be even more compacted by describing a mathematical model I with M for mass term, B for boundary term, S for stiffness and F for forcing term: \begin{equation}
I_I(M) - I_I(B) + I_I(S) = I_I(F) = \sum_{P \in \{M,B,S,F\}} I_I(P) = 0
\label{eq:abstract weak form}
\end{equation}  \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. \par 

To solve \ref{eq:abstract weak form} the Galerkin approach is used, the test function w and solution u are approximated by $\ \phi$, which are a set of functions from the same space \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. Any discrete function, for example displacement can be approximated this way: \begin{equation}
  g(x) \approx \sum_{j=1}^{N} F_j \phi_j(x), 
  \label{eq:galerkin displacement}
\end{equation} where $\ F_j$ is the expansion of the basis and g is the continous function which is the approximation \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. The derivatives of a function can then be approximated by the derivatives of $\ \phi$. \par

To evaluate the integrals in \ref{eq:abstract weak form}, numerical quadrature is used \cite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. A certain number of intergration weights W are combined, g(x) and the derivative of g(x) are evaluated at a set of integration points $\ x_i$ \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. This resluts in \begin{equation}
\int_G g(x)d^n x \approx \sum_{i=1}^{M}W_i^G \sum_{j=1}^{N}F_j\phi_j(x_i)
  \label{eq:weighted weak form}
\end{equation} \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. In FE, an element-specific reference coordinate system $\ \theta$ is used. In this thesis, the domain consists of a snow layer and an air layer, which means there is a sharp discountinuity in model parameters. As \cite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019} state, the model parameters and strain field are not differentiable. This is why a smooth polynomial basis of the governing equations over the whole domain is not appropriate, the basis B needs to consist of piecewise functions G defined on the individual elements \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. The integration weights and basis function derivatives used, according to \cite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}, are pre-tabulated in the element-specific coordinate system $\ \theta$. To transfrom the integrals and derivaties to physical coordinates x, so the coordinates of the snow extent in this case, the element-specific Jacobian $\ \frac{\delta x}{\delta \theta}$ is used \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. Equations \ref{eq:galerkin displacement} and \ref{eq:weighted weak form} and their derivatives become: \begin{equation}
g(x) \approx \sum_{N}^{j=1} F_j \phi_j(\theta(x))
\label{eq:first galerkin}
\end{equation} and \begin{equation}
  \nabla g(x) \approx \sum_{j=1}^{N} F_j \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \phi_j (\theta(x))\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial x}
  \label{eq:second galerkin}
\end{equation} \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. In equations \ref{eq:first galerkin} and \ref{eq:second galerkin}, the function space V is approximated as $\ V_h \approx V$ in a previous step, this means the accuracy of the approximation is dependent on how well the original function space is approximated \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. To make calculations over the whole domain G, the contributions from each element need to be summed: \begin{equation} 
  \int_{G_e} g(x)\,\mathrm{d}^n x \approx \sum_{i=1}^{M} W_i^{G_e} \sum_{j=1}^{N} F_j\,\phi_j(\theta(x_i))\left|\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\right|_{x=x_i}^n
  \label{eq:domain integral}
\end{equation} for the volume and \begin{equation}
  \int_{\partial G_e} g(x)\,\mathrm{d}^{n-1} x \approx \sum_{i=1}^{M} W_i^{\partial G_e} \sum_{j=1}^{N} F_j\,\phi_j(\theta(x_i))\left|\frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\right|_{x=x_i}^{n-1}
  \label{eq:surface integral}
\end{equation} for the surface of the domain \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. \par

The dependence on the reference coordinate system is described by E, which is a model containing the physical coordinate to reference coordinate transformation, in other words, the determinant of the Jacobian matrix and its inverse \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. Using basis B of the polynomial functions of the approximation $\ V_h$, which are piecewise per-element because of possible discontinuities, the abstract form in \ref{eq:abstract weak form} becomes \begin{equation}
  \sum_{P \in \{M,B,S,F\}} I_I(P,B(E)) = 0
  \label{eq:abstract approximation}
\end{equation} \parencite{afanasievModularFlexibleSpectralelement2019}. This means that fields such as displacement and velocity are calculated in this way. From these fields, the data from the model in section \ref{sec:czm_methods} is obtained and plotted. 

\section{From Particles to Waves: Translating MPM to SE}
\label{sec:from_mpm_to_se}

The material point simulations of the PST performed by \parencite{gaumeDynamicAnticrackPropagation2018,trottetTransitionSubRayleighAnticrack2022} result in the recorded stresses in the model according to \cite{gaumeInvestigatingReleaseFlow2019}. The output of the model is particle velocity, the stress tensor, strain and the J-value, which distinguished between elastic and plastic deformation. The J-value is used as a filter to remove any plastic deformation. The filtering by deformation also ensures that any waves emitted by the cracking in the model are filtered out. This ensures that the data only contains the crack itself so this data can be input into SALVUS. \par

 This results in the stretch factor $\ \epsilon$ comprised of the material properties $\ C_{ij}$ such as Young's modulus, Lamé parameters and Poisson ratio as well as the stress matrix $\ \sigma$: \begin{equation}
  \epsilon = C_{ij}\sigma
\end{equation} \parencite{holzapfelNonlinearSolidMechanics2010}. The equation can be rewritten to obtain the stresses in different directions, in the xx direction for example: \begin{equation}
  \sigma_{xx} = (\lambda + 2\mu)\epsilon_{xx} + \lambda \epsilon_{yy}
\end{equation} \parencite{kAkiRichards19801981}. When scaling this with the particle volume, the individual moment tensor components can be obtained using \begin{equation}
  M_{ij} = \sigma_{ij} V_p.
\end{equation} \par

These moment tensor components can then be input into SALVUS as custom source time functions. The waveforms are obtained via the amplitude of the different moment tensor components at different times. \par

\section{Simulation of DAS data}

To compare the results obtained in the SALVUS simulations to field DAS data, the numerical outputs have to be transformed. The backscattered delay of a laser pulse along a straigth cable is proportional to the integrated strain tensor components along the gauge length \parencite{noeFrontiersFullwaveformSeismology2025}. This yields the standard expression for the gauge-averaged DAS strain at a channel position: \begin{equation}
\bar{\epsilon}_{\text{DAS}}(s_c, t) = \frac{1}{l} \int_{s_c - \frac{l}{2}}^{s_c + \frac{l}{2}} \mathbf{e}^T(s) \boldsymbol{\epsilon}(s, t) \mathbf{e}(s) \, ds,
\end{equation} \parencite{noeFrontiersFullwaveformSeismology2025}. \par

To model a physically realistic laser pulse with a tapered spatial intensity profile, the classical boxcar averaging window is generalized to a continuous, weighted spatial convolution according to \cite{noeFrontiersFullwaveformSeismology2025}:

\begin{equation}
\bar{\epsilon}_{\text{DAS}}(x, t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} w(s) \cdot \epsilon_{xx}(x - s, t) \, ds,
\end{equation}

where $w(s)$ represents the normalized Gaussian spatial weighting kernel configured via the Pyber engine (\cite{noeFrontiersFullwaveformSeismology2025}, located on github), satisfying the amplitude-preserving condition:

\begin{equation}
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} w(s) \, ds = 1
\end{equation} \parencite{noeFrontiersFullwaveformSeismology2025}.

On the discrete numerical grid output by SALVUS, this forward problem maps to a discrete 1D spatial convolution over $N_g$ sampling points within the gauge length window:

\begin{equation}
\bar{\epsilon}_{\text{DAS}}(x_i, t) \approx \sum_{k=-N_g/2}^{N_g/2} w_k \cdot \epsilon_{xx}(x_{i-k}, t).
\end{equation}

Because spatial convolution and spatial differentiation are both linear operations, they strictly commute. This linear commutativity is exploited numerically to apply the pulse filtering directly to the raw horizontal displacement field $u_x$ prior to differentiation:

\begin{equation}
\bar{\epsilon}_{\text{DAS}}(x_i, t) = \sum_{k} w_k \cdot \left( \frac{\partial u_x(x_{i-k}, t)}{\partial x} \right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left[ \sum_{k} w_k \cdot u_x(x_{i-k}, t) \right].
\end{equation}

Finally, the simulated DAS strain rate is acquired by evaluating the temporal derivative of the stabilized strain field:

\begin{equation}
\dot{\bar{\epsilon}}_{\text{DAS}}(x_i, t) = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left[ \bar{\epsilon}_{\text{DAS}}(x_i, t) \right].
\end{equation}
