\begin{frame}[allowframebreaks]{Words I will propably say a lot explained}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Slab: Part of the snowpack which breaks off and slides downhill as a cohesive unit (the avalanche itself, I'm looking at dry-snow slab avalanches) \parencite{schweizerSnowAvalancheFormation2003}
        \item Weak layer: Layer with different mechanical properties than the rest of the snow, cracks usually propagate in this layer because it's weaker than the rest of the snowpack \parencite{schweizerSnowAvalancheFormation2003}
        \item Mode I, Mode II and Mode III cracks: cracks opening as a response to stress in different directions: 
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{../figures/fracture_modes.png}
    \captionof{figure}{Fracture modes according to \cite{philippEffectsMechanicalLayering2013}}
    \item Anticrack: weak layer collapses under loading, slab starts bending, collapse and cracks caused by bending propagate \parencite{gaumeDynamicAnticrackPropagation2018}
    \item Sub-Rayleigh: Cracks that propagate at speeds lower than the shear wave speed in snow \parencite{trottetTransitionSubRayleighAnticrack2022}
    \item Supershear: Cracks propagate faster than shear wave speed in snow \parencite{trottetTransitionSubRayleighAnticrack2022}
    \end{itemize}
    
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Overview of Models}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Model domain 400m wide, 3m high
        \item Absorbing boundary conditions 30m thick along top and both sides of domain
        \item No ABC at bottom because of theoretical snow interface
        \item 1.5m snow layer, 1.5m air layer
        \item Multiple source versions: single source, source line with sources firing with a time delay (moving source) and rupture front
        \item Sources buried in snow (like crack)
        \item For moving source: sub-rayleigh and supershear speeds
        \item 10Hz central frequency ricker source wavelet
    \end{itemize}
    
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{My latest creation: the cohesive zone model (CZM)}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Model based on the cohesive zone model by \cite{mahajanModelingInterfacialCrack2008}
        \item In CZM, fracturing is a gradual process, separation of the crack faces takes place over an extended crack tip, the cohesive zone \parencite{mahajanModelingInterfacialCrack2008}
        \item Resistance to opening by cohesive traction \parencite{mahajanModelingInterfacialCrack2008}
        \item In other words: there is a zone ahead of the crack tip, which already has some damage of the bonds
        \item Cracks represented as a fraction between different crack modes (Mode I-III)
        \item Caused by a a fault zone: starts at x=30m and ends at x=270m, 0.5m depth (middle of snow layer)
        \item Sources every 10cm firing with a time delay (dependend on supershear vs sub-rayleigh)
    \end{itemize}  
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[allowframebreaks]{Possible interpretations of space-time plots}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Sub-Rayleigh and Supershear case look very different (yay!)
        \item Blue-red-blue arrival before the main wavefront could be flexural wave (bending of the slab near the source)
        \item Blue first: negative velocity --> source in negative y (slab is pushed down by the source)
        \item Red next: because of elastic rebound of slab 
        \item Blue again: oscillation in source direction after rebound
        \item This can be seen in vx for sub-rayleigh and strain for supershear
        \item In sub-rayleigh, waves travel slower than the flexural waves, which is very noticeable when looking at vx
        \item In supershear vx, rupture arrives with flexural waves 
        \item Flexural waves can be seen in strain supershear plot: because strain is spatial derivative, which acts as a high-pass and amplifies flexural waves 
        \item This is because the derivative acts as a multiplication with $ \ j\omega $, which is dependent on frequency: the higher the frequency, the greater $ \ |j\omega| $, which means there is amplification of high frequencies \parencite{oppenheimSignalsSystems1997}
        \item Supershear case: Strong first arrivals, weak arrivals at later times
        \item "Reflection" seen in bottom rigth corner is rupture arrest front (energy could increase because rupture stops at a point)
        \item This is only visible in supershear case because there is constructive interference between the rupture front and the wavefronts because they move at similar speeds in supershear
    \end{itemize}
    
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Was it all just a dream?}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Source wavelet pulse (green) matches traces at both reciever locations
        \item Far trace (200m) has stronger coda noise in vy: could be rupture arrest? 
        \item All PSD have a clear peak at the source frequency (10Hz)
        \item F-k plot shows high energies at same slope of rupture speed: model takes the correct speed
        \item Trailing (s) wave can also be seen (line with similar slope to black line)
        \item Waves propagate in both directions (in rupture direction as well as against it)
        \item Probably not a dream, my model might be correct!
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
